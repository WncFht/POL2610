## Part 1: Full English Presentation Script (Approx. 1600 words)

**[Slide 1: Title Page & Introduction]**
**Time Allocation: 1.0 Minute (0:00 - 1:00)**

Good morning, Professor and classmates. Welcome to my presentation. Today, I am going to take you on a deep dive into the shifting geopolitical landscape of China's periphery. Specifically, we will be examining the emerging regional order in Northeast Asia. Our core focus will be on how middle powers in this region—namely, Japan and South Korea—are actively navigating the intensifying great power competition between the United States and China. 

We often hear in the media that the world is slipping into a new Cold War, divided neatly into opposing blocs. However, through the lens of International Relations theory, I will argue that this narrative is overly simplistic. Instead, Japan and South Korea are constructing what I call an "Uneven Dual-Trilateral Structure." By examining this structure, we can clearly see that these countries are not blindly picking sides; rather, they are executing a highly sophisticated strategy of diplomatic and security hedging. Let us explore how this is happening.

**[Slide 2: The Empirical Puzzle]**
**Time Allocation: 1.5 Minutes (1:00 - 2:30)**

Let’s begin our analysis with a fascinating empirical puzzle. If we look closely at the diplomatic timeline of Northeast Asia from late 2023 to early 2025, we observe two seemingly contradictory trends occurring at the exact same time. 

Look at the upper track on this timeline. We see a rapid, unprecedented military integration between Washington, Tokyo, and Seoul. This includes the historic Camp David Summit in August 2023, the upgrading of extended deterrence frameworks in July 2024, and most recently, the fourth US-ROK Nuclear Consultative Group, or NCG, meeting in January 2025. 

However, look at the lower track. At the very moment when you would expect Northeast Asia to fracture completely, something entirely different happens. In May 2024, after four and a half years of complete stagnation, the China-Japan-ROK trilateral summit was fully revived in Seoul. This was recently followed by a trilateral Foreign Ministers' meeting in March 2025. 

This presents our core puzzle: If Japan and South Korea are rapidly and deeply binding themselves to the US military bloc, why are they simultaneously investing political capital to rebuild institutionalized diplomatic channels with China? 

**[Slide 3: Limits of Traditional Alliance Theory]**
**Time Allocation: 2.0 Minutes (2:30 - 4:30)**

To answer this puzzle, we must first turn to traditional Alliance Theory, specifically the "Balance of Threat" framework developed by the prominent neorealist scholar Stephen Walt. Traditional structural realism suggests that when states are confronted with a severe external threat, they have two primary choices. 

The first choice is **"Balancing"**. Briefly speaking, balancing means forming an alliance with others to pool capabilities against a prevailing threat. The second choice is **"Bandwagoning"**, which means aligning yourself directly with the source of the threat to appease it and avoid being attacked. 

If we strictly apply Walt's theory, the US-Japan-ROK military integration appears to be a textbook, classic case of "balancing." Tokyo and Seoul feel threatened by North Korea's nuclear program and China's military modernization, so they balance against them by aligning with the United States. 

However, there is a fundamental flaw in applying this theory to today's Northeast Asia: balancing assumes a binary choice. You are either a friend or a foe. Traditional balancing completely fails to explain the May 2024 China-Japan-ROK Summit. If Japan and South Korea are purely balancing against China, why would they seek to institutionalize high-level, cooperative agreements with the very power they are supposed to be containing? We need a better theoretical tool.

**[Slide 4: The Reality: Middle Power Hedging]**
**Time Allocation: 2.0 Minutes (4:30 - 6:30)**

This brings us to the work of scholars like Evelyn Goh and the concept of the "economics-security nexus." To understand Japan and South Korea, we must categorize them correctly: they are **"Middle Powers."** In IR terminology, middle powers are states that are not global superpowers, but possess significant regional influence, robust economies, and a deep reliance on international stability.

Because these middle powers rely heavily on the United States for their military survival, but rely equally heavily on China for their economic prosperity and supply chain stability, they face an excruciating dilemma. Therefore, their actual strategic behavior is not binary balancing; it is **"Hedging."** 

Hedging is a sophisticated strategy of avoiding taking sides entirely. It involves spreading risks by maintaining positive, working relations with competing great powers simultaneously. What we are witnessing is a strategy of "bifurcated alignment." Japan and South Korea are deliberately splitting the regional architecture into two separate tracks. They are pushing all "hard security" issues onto the US-led track, while desperately trying to keep economics, trade, and diplomatic communication strictly on the China-led track. 

**[Slide 5: Track 1 Theory: Deterrence Minilateralism]**
**Time Allocation: 1.5 Minutes (6:30 - 8:00)**

Let us now deeply analyze Track 1: The US-Japan-ROK framework. The theoretical essence of this track is **"Minilateralism."** Unlike broad, inclusive, and often slow-moving multilateral organizations like the United Nations or ASEAN, minilateralism refers to exclusive, highly targeted, and flexible cooperation among a very small group of states sharing a specific objective. 

Here, the specific objective is **"Extended Deterrence"**—which basically means the American nuclear umbrella. It is the commitment by the US to use its full range of military capabilities, including nuclear weapons, to defend its allies. 

As scholar Michito Tsuruoka points out, modern deterrence in Northeast Asia is evolving. It no longer relies just on forward-deployed troops. Instead, it increasingly relies on *visible institutionalization*. Why? Because allies are terrified of domestic political shifts—like a new, unpredictable administration in Washington or Seoul. By institutionalizing their cooperation, they create bureaucratic inertia that makes the alliance much harder for future politicians to dismantle.

**[Slide 6: Track 1 in Action: US-Japan-ROK Institutionalization]**
**Time Allocation: 1.5 Minutes (8:00 - 9:30)**

How does this theory of "visible institutionalization" play out in recent facts? Let's trace the timeline. 

In August 2023, the Camp David Summit produced the "Commitment to Consult." This was revolutionary because it turned ad-hoc, temporary crisis responses into a mandatory, formalized routine. Moving to July 2024, the three nations signed a joint declaration that normalized and standardized multi-domain military exercises. 

In December 2024, the US and Japan issued explicit extended deterrence guidelines, cementing their operational coordination. And most recently, in January 2025, the fourth US-ROK Nuclear Consultative Group (NCG) explicitly advanced conventional-nuclear military integration. 

Do you notice the overarching trend here? These countries are no longer just issuing political rhetoric. They are writing standard operating procedures, sharing real-time missile warning data, and embedding their command structures. This is the ultimate materialization of a rigid, hard-security minilateral bloc designed strictly for deterrence.

**[Slide 7: Track 2 Theory: Functional Trilateralism]**
**Time Allocation: 1.5 Minutes (9:30 - 11:00)**

Now, if Track 1 is entirely about preparing for war and worst-case scenarios, Track 2 is about managing peace and daily existence. The China-Japan-ROK framework represents the theory of **"Functional Trilateralism."** 

Drawing on the functionalist approach outlined by scholars like Jihwan Hwang and Sung-han Kim, we must distinguish between two types of politics. **"High Politics"** refers to issues concerning national security, military power, territorial disputes, and state survival. **"Low Politics"** refers to non-controversial, functional issues like trade, environmental protection, and public health. 

Functionalism argues that when High Politics is completely deadlocked by mistrust, states can still build robust, meaningful cooperation through Low Politics. You do not need to resolve your military paranoia or historical grievances before you can cooperate on preventing a pandemic or managing a free trade zone. Low politics serves as the glue that keeps relationships from completely shattering.

**[Slide 8: Track 2 in Action: The Diplomatic Safety Valve]**
**Time Allocation: 1.5 Minutes (11:00 - 12:30)**

We see this functionalist theory perfectly executed in the recent diplomatic events of Track 2. In May 2024, at the Seoul Summit, the leaders of China, Japan, and South Korea issued a joint declaration. If you read the text, it entirely sidesteps collective security obligations, military transparency, or territorial disputes. 

Instead, what were the core pillars of their agreement? They were climate change, digital transformation, managing aging populations, public health, and disaster relief. When the Foreign Ministers met again in March 2025, they reaffirmed this exact same functional track. 

This is not an accident. By deliberately keeping the agenda "narrow, specific, and non-military," Japan and South Korea are utilizing the CJK mechanism as a diplomatic "safety valve." It guarantees an institutionalized channel of communication with Beijing, effectively preventing Northeast Asia from completely deteriorating into a zero-contact, mutually isolated Cold War environment. 

**[Slide 9: The Uneven Dual-Trilateral Structure]**
**Time Allocation: 1.5 Minutes (12:30 - 14:00)**

Synthesizing both the theoretical frameworks and the empirical evidence, we arrive at the core argument and conclusion of this presentation. Northeast Asia is not being neatly divided into two symmetrical, opposing alliances. Instead, a complex, **"Uneven Dual-Trilateral Structure"** is taking shape. 

Why is it "uneven"? Because the two tracks are not competing to do the same job; they serve fundamentally different purposes. The US-Japan-ROK track is built to manage deterrence, military coordination, and existential crises. The China-Japan-ROK track is built to manage economic interdependence, functional cooperation, and daily diplomatic buffering. 

These two tracks exist in parallel because middle powers *need* both. Japan and South Korea require Track 1 to survive in a dangerous security environment, but they absolutely require Track 2 to thrive economically and avoid an unwanted catastrophic confrontation with their largest neighbor.

**[Slide 10: Conclusion & Implications for China]**
**Time Allocation: 1.0 Minute (14:00 - 15:00)**

To conclude, what are the broader implications of this structure for regional order, and specifically for China's peripheral diplomacy? 

First, the regional order is increasingly defined by **"Issue-layering."** This means that security logic and economic logic are decoupling in their institutional forms. The region operates on different layers depending on the issue at hand.

Second, for China, this dual structure presents both a reality check and a strategic opportunity. While Beijing is finding it increasingly difficult to prevent Japan and South Korea from aligning with Washington on hard security, China has successfully utilized the functional Track 2. By actively investing political energy into the 2024 and 2025 CJK mechanisms, China is effectively preventing the hardening of Northeast Asia into a singular, US-dominated security architecture. As long as Track 2 functions, China retains crucial institutional space, and the diplomatic buffer remains wide open.
